# Indian History — A Complete Overview

India has one of the oldest, richest, and most continuous civilizations in the world, spanning over 5,000 years. Its history is marked by remarkable achievements in philosophy, science, trade, art, governance, and spiritual thought. From the banks of the Indus River to the launch pads of ISRO, India's journey is one of the most extraordinary stories in human civilization.

---

## 1. Ancient India (3300 BCE – 600 CE)

### 1.1 Indus Valley Civilization (3300–1300 BCE)

The Indus Valley Civilization (IVC), also known as the Harappan Civilization, is one of the world's three earliest urban cultures, alongside Mesopotamia and Ancient Egypt. It flourished along the Indus River and its tributaries in present-day Pakistan and northwest India.

**Key Features:**
- Major cities: Harappa, Mohenjo-Daro, Dholavira, Lothal, Kalibangan, and Rakhigarhi.
- Advanced urban planning with grid-pattern streets and two-storey brick houses.
- Underground drainage and sewage systems — far ahead of contemporary civilizations.
- Standardized weights, measures, and fired bricks.
- Evidence of trade with Mesopotamia (seals and goods found in both regions).
- The Great Bath at Mohenjo-Daro suggests ritual bathing practices.
- The civilization had a script that remains undeciphered to this day.

**Decline:** Around 1900–1300 BCE, the civilization gradually declined, possibly due to climate change, drying of the Saraswati River, tectonic shifts, or migration of people eastward toward the Gangetic plains.

---

### 1.2 Vedic Period (1500–500 BCE)

The Vedic Age followed the decline of the IVC and saw the migration or arrival of Indo-Aryan-speaking peoples into northern India. This period is divided into the Early Vedic Period (Rigvedic) and the Later Vedic Period.

**Key Developments:**
- Composition of the four Vedas: Rigveda, Samaveda, Yajurveda, and Atharvaveda — among the oldest religious and literary texts in the world.
- The Upanishads and Aranyakas explored deep philosophical questions about the nature of reality, the soul (Atman), and the universal spirit (Brahman).
- The Sanskrit language developed into a sophisticated literary and liturgical medium.
- The varna system (social stratification) emerged: Brahmins (priests), Kshatriyas (warriors), Vaishyas (traders), and Shudras (labourers).
- Epic literature — the Mahabharata and Ramayana — has its foundational roots in oral traditions of this era.
- Early agricultural communities settled along the Gangetic plains.
- The Vedic people practised a polytheistic religion centred on fire rituals (yajnas) and nature deities like Indra, Agni, and Varuna.

---

### 1.3 Mahajanapadas and the Rise of New Religions (600–321 BCE)

By 600 BCE, northern India had evolved into 16 powerful republics and monarchies called **Mahajanapadas**, including Magadha, Kosala, Vajji, Kashi, Anga, Avanti, and Gandhara.

**Key Developments:**
- Magadha emerged as the most powerful state, eventually forming the base for the first empire.
- Cities like Pataliputra, Vaishali, and Taxila became centers of commerce and learning.
- **Buddhism:** Founded by Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha, c. 563–483 BCE) in the kingdom of Kosala. He attained enlightenment under the Bodhi Tree at Bodh Gaya and delivered his first sermon at Sarnath. Buddhism preached the Four Noble Truths, the Eightfold Path, non-violence (ahimsa), and the rejection of caste hierarchy.
- **Jainism:** Founded or reformed by Vardhamana Mahavira (c. 599–527 BCE), the 24th Tirthankara. Jainism emphasized extreme non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, and non-attachment. Both movements challenged Brahmanical authority and ritualism.
- The Persian Achaemenid Empire under Darius I invaded and annexed the northwestern regions (Gandhara and Sindh) around 518 BCE.
- Alexander the Great invaded northwestern India in 326 BCE, defeating King Porus at the Battle of the Hydaspes. Though he turned back due to his army's reluctance, his campaign opened up Indo-Greek cultural exchange.

---

### 1.4 Maurya Empire (321–185 BCE)

The Maurya Empire was the first pan-Indian empire, uniting most of the subcontinent under a single administration.

**Chandragupta Maurya (321–297 BCE):**
- Founded the empire with the strategic guidance of Chanakya (Kautilya), the prime minister and author of the Arthashastra — an ancient treatise on statecraft, economic policy, and military strategy.
- Defeated the Nanda dynasty and expelled the Greek Seleucid forces from northwestern India.
- Later abdicated the throne and embraced Jainism; according to tradition, he fasted to death at Shravanabelagola.

**Bindusara (297–273 BCE):**
- Expanded the empire southward into the Deccan plateau.
- Maintained diplomatic relations with the Seleucid Empire and Egypt.

**Ashoka the Great (268–232 BCE):**
- The greatest emperor of the Maurya dynasty and one of the most remarkable rulers in world history.
- After the brutal Kalinga War (c. 261 BCE), which resulted in massive casualties and suffering, Ashoka underwent a profound transformation and embraced Buddhism.
- He promoted dhamma (moral law), non-violence, religious tolerance, welfare of people and animals, and ethical governance.
- Erected numerous pillars and rock edicts throughout the empire, inscribed with his principles.
- Sent Buddhist missionaries across Asia — to Sri Lanka, Central Asia, and Southeast Asia — spreading the religion widely.
- The Lion Capital at Sarnath, erected by Ashoka, is India's national emblem. The Ashoka Chakra on it is featured on the Indian national flag.
- After his death, the empire fragmented and fell to the Shunga dynasty in 185 BCE.

---

### 1.5 Post-Mauryan Period (185 BCE – 320 CE)

After the Mauryas, India saw a succession of dynasties and foreign invasions:

- **Shunga Dynasty (185–73 BCE):** Revived Brahmanical Hinduism; notable for the construction of the Sanchi Stupa.
- **Kanva Dynasty (73–28 BCE):** Succeeded the Shungas in Magadha.
- **Indo-Greek Kingdoms:** Greek rulers like Menander I (Milinda) ruled parts of northwest India; some converted to Buddhism.
- **Kushana Empire (c. 30–375 CE):** Founded by Kujula Kadphises; reached its peak under **Kanishka the Great**, a patron of Buddhism. The Silk Road trade flourished. Gandhara art, a fusion of Greek and Buddhist styles, produced iconic Buddha sculptures.
- **Satavahana Dynasty (c. 230 BCE–220 CE):** Dominated the Deccan; important for trade and the spread of Buddhism southward.

---

### 1.6 Gupta Empire — The Golden Age (320–550 CE)

The Gupta Empire is considered India's Golden Age — a period of unparalleled intellectual, artistic, and scientific achievement.

**Key Rulers:**
- **Chandragupta I (320–335 CE):** Founded the empire.
- **Samudragupta (335–375 CE):** A brilliant military commander who expanded the empire across most of India; called the "Napoleon of India."
- **Chandragupta II / Vikramaditya (375–415 CE):** The empire reached its zenith. His court hosted the Navaratnas (Nine Gems), including the poet-playwright Kalidasa.

**Achievements of the Gupta Period:**
- **Mathematics & Astronomy:** Aryabhata calculated the value of pi, proposed that the Earth rotates on its axis, and accurately calculated the solar year. Brahmagupta later developed rules for zero and negative numbers.
- **Literature:** Kalidasa wrote Shakuntala, Meghaduta, and Raghuvamsha — masterpieces of Sanskrit literature.
- **Medicine:** Charaka (Charaka Samhita) and Sushruta (Sushruta Samhita) codified Ayurvedic medicine and surgical procedures. Sushruta is considered the father of surgery.
- **Architecture & Art:** Elaborate cave temples, including early phases of Ajanta and Ellora, were developed. The iron pillar of Delhi from this era shows extraordinary metallurgical skill — it has remained rust-free for over 1,600 years.
- The Nalanda University (formalized later) had its roots in this period and became one of the greatest centers of learning in the ancient world.

**Decline:** Invasions by the Huna (White Huns) from Central Asia weakened the empire from the late 5th century. After Skandagupta's reign, the empire fragmented into regional kingdoms.

---

## 2. Medieval India (600–1757 CE)

### 2.1 Post-Gupta Regional Kingdoms (600–1200 CE)

After the Gupta decline, India fragmented into many regional powers. Some of the most notable:

**Harsha's Empire (606–647 CE):**
- Emperor Harshavardhana of Kanauj briefly reunited much of northern India. His court was visited by the Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang (Hiuen Tsang), whose accounts provide valuable historical records.

**Pallava Dynasty (275–897 CE) — South India:**
- Based in Kanchipuram; known for Dravidian temple architecture (e.g., Mahabalipuram Shore Temple and Rathas). Patronized Tamil literature and learning.

**Chalukya Dynasties (543–1189 CE):**
- The Badami Chalukyas, Vengi Chalukyas, and Western Chalukyas were major powers in the Deccan. They developed the Vesara style of temple architecture.

**Rashtrakuta Dynasty (753–982 CE):**
- Dominant Deccan power; famous for commissioning the Kailasa Temple at Ellora — carved entirely out of a single rock, one of the greatest architectural achievements in the world.

**Chola Empire (9th–13th Century CE):**
- One of the longest-ruling dynasties in world history; originated in Tamil Nadu.
- **Rajaraja Chola I** and **Rajendra Chola I** expanded the empire across South India, Sri Lanka, the Maldives, and Southeast Asia through naval power.
- Built the magnificent **Brihadeeswarar Temple** (Thanjavur) — a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
- Developed an efficient village-level administrative system.
- Promoted Tamil literature (Sangam Age texts), music, and Bharatanatyam dance.
- Trade with China, Southeast Asia, and the Arab world flourished.

**Pala Dynasty (8th–12th Century CE) — Bengal:**
- Great patrons of Buddhism; supported the Nalanda and Vikramashila universities, which attracted scholars from across Asia.

---

### 2.2 Rajput Kingdoms (7th–12th Century CE)

The Rajputs were a collection of warrior clans who dominated much of northern and central India. Key kingdoms included the Paramaras, Chandellas (builders of the Khajuraho temples), Chahamanas (Chauhans), and Pratiharas.

- **Prithviraj Chauhan III** was the most celebrated Rajput king; he defeated Muhammad Ghori at the First Battle of Tarain (1191) but was defeated at the Second Battle of Tarain (1192), opening northern India to Islamic rule.

---

### 2.3 Islamic Invasions and the Delhi Sultanate (1206–1526 CE)

**Early Islamic Incursions:**
- **Muhammad bin Qasim** conquered Sindh in 711–712 CE — the first significant Arab presence in India.
- **Mahmud of Ghazni** conducted 17 raids into India between 1000–1027 CE, famously looting the Somnath Temple.
- **Muhammad Ghori** made permanent conquests after 1192 CE, laying the foundation for Muslim rule in Delhi.

**The Delhi Sultanate** was ruled by five successive dynasties:

| Dynasty | Period | Notable Rulers |
|---|---|---|
| Slave (Mamluk) | 1206–1290 | Qutb ud-Din Aibak, Iltutmish, Razia Sultana, Balban |
| Khilji | 1290–1320 | Jalal ud-Din Khilji, Alauddin Khilji |
| Tughlaq | 1320–1414 | Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq, Muhammad bin Tughlaq, Firoz Shah Tughlaq |
| Sayyid | 1414–1451 | Khizr Khan |
| Lodi | 1451–1526 | Bahlul Lodi, Ibrahim Lodi |

**Key Developments:**
- **Qutb ud-Din Aibak** built the Qutb Minar in Delhi, one of India's most iconic monuments.
- **Iltutmish** consolidated the Sultanate and became the first Sultan to receive recognition from the Abbasid Caliph.
- **Razia Sultana (1236–1240):** The first and only female ruler of the Delhi Sultanate.
- **Alauddin Khilji (1296–1316):** The most powerful Khilji ruler. He expanded the Sultanate across most of the subcontinent, implemented strict market controls, repelled multiple Mongol invasions, and conquered the Deccan through his general Malik Kafur.
- **Muhammad bin Tughlaq:** Attempted bold but disastrous administrative experiments, including shifting the capital from Delhi to Daulatabad and introducing token currency.
- **Timur's invasion (1398):** The Central Asian conqueror Timur (Tamerlane) sacked Delhi, massacring thousands and accelerating the Sultanate's decline.
- **Babur's victory at the First Battle of Panipat (1526)** ended the Lodi dynasty and established the Mughal Empire.

**Cultural Impact:** The Sultanate period saw the emergence of Indo-Islamic architecture, the Sufi movement (which promoted spiritual devotion and had a profound humanizing influence on Indian society), the Bhakti movement (devotional Hindu poetry by saints like Kabir, Mirabai, Tukaram, Ramdas), and the development of Urdu as a language.

---

### 2.4 Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646 CE)

Founded by Harihara I and Bukka Raya I in 1336, the Vijayanagara Empire was the last great Hindu empire in South India, established as a bulwark against the northward expansion of the Delhi Sultanate.

- At its peak, it was one of the largest and wealthiest empires in the world.
- **Krishnadevaraya (1509–1529)** was the greatest ruler — a brilliant military commander and scholar who composed the Telugu classic Amuktamalyada. His court was adorned with eight celebrated scholars called Ashtadiggajas.
- The capital city of Vijayanagara (modern Hampi, Karnataka) was one of the world's largest cities, with a population estimated at 500,000.
- The empire was a great patron of Telugu, Kannada, Tamil, and Sanskrit literature, music, and temple architecture (Hoysala, Dravidian styles).
- **Battle of Talikota (1565):** A coalition of Deccan Sultanates (Bijapur, Bidar, Ahmadnagar, Golconda) defeated and destroyed Vijayanagara. The capital was razed, though regional governors continued to rule for another century.

---

### 2.5 Mughal Empire (1526–1857 CE)


**Babur (1526–1530):**
- Descendant of Timur and Genghis Khan; founded the empire after defeating Ibrahim Lodi at the First Battle of Panipat (1526) using artillery — a decisive technological advantage.


**Akbar (1556–1605):**
- The greatest of the Mughal emperors. Came to the throne at age 13; under regent Bairam Khan, he defeated Hemu at the Second Battle of Panipat (1556).

---

### 2.6 Maratha Empire (1674–1818 CE)

**Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj (1630–1680):**
- The founder of the Maratha kingdom, born in the Bhonsle clan in the Western Ghats of Maharashtra.
- A master of guerrilla warfare (ganimi kava), he carved out an independent Hindu kingdom from the Mughal and Bijapur Sultanate territories.
- Built a powerful navy — one of the first Indian rulers to do so — to counter Portuguese and British sea power.
- Established a just and efficient administration with a council of eight ministers (Ashtapradhan).
- Coronated as Chhatrapati (sovereign) in 1674 at Raigad Fort.

**Later Maratha Expansion:**
- Under the **Peshwas** (prime ministers), especially **Peshwa Bajirao I (1720–1740)**, the Marathas expanded dramatically — at their peak controlling much of the subcontinent from Attock (Pakistan) in the north to Tamil Nadu in the south.
- **Battle of Panipat (1761):** The Marathas suffered a catastrophic defeat at the hands of Ahmad Shah Durrani, ending their dream of a pan-Indian empire.
- The Marathas reorganized as a confederacy under the Peshwa, with powerful chiefs like the Scindias (Gwalior), Holkars (Indore), Bhonsles (Nagpur), and Gaekwads (Baroda).
- Three Anglo-Maratha Wars (1775–1818) ended with British victory and annexation of all Maratha territories.

---

### 2.7 Sikh Empire (1799–1849 CE)

**Guru Nanak Dev Ji (1469–1539)** founded Sikhism in Punjab, preaching equality, devotion to one God, and rejection of caste and ritualism. The ten Sikh Gurus, from Guru Nanak to **Guru Gobind Singh Ji (1666–1708)**, developed the faith. Guru Gobind Singh established the Khalsa (the community of baptized Sikhs) in 1699 and gave Sikhs the five Ks (Panj Kakars).

**Maharaja Ranjit Singh (1780–1839):**
- United the various Sikh misls (confederacies) and founded the Sikh Empire, ruling from Lahore.
- Built one of the strongest armies in Asia, trained by European officers.
- Renowned for his secular and just administration; Hindus, Muslims, and Sikhs all held senior positions.
- Recovered the Koh-i-Noor diamond, which became part of his treasury.
- After his death in 1839, succession disputes weakened the empire.

**Anglo-Sikh Wars:**
- First Anglo-Sikh War (1845–46): British victory led to loss of territory.
- Second Anglo-Sikh War (1848–49): Complete British annexation of Punjab. The Koh-i-Noor diamond was taken and later presented to Queen Victoria.

---

## 3. Colonial India and the Freedom Struggle (1757–1947)

### 3.1 Arrival of Europeans

- **Portuguese (1498):** Vasco da Gama arrived at Calicut (Kozhikode) in 1498, establishing the first direct sea trade route between Europe and India. The Portuguese established colonies at Goa (1510), Daman, and Diu.
- **Dutch:** Established trading posts in the 17th century but were eventually eclipsed.
- **French:** Founded settlements at Pondicherry, Chandernagore, and Mahe; competed with the British for dominance; lost influence after the Carnatic Wars (1744–1763).
- **British East India Company:** Founded in 1600; established its first factory at Surat (1608), then Madras (1639), Bombay (1661), and Calcutta (1690).

---

### 3.2 Rise of British Power

**Battle of Plassey (1757):**
- Robert Clive of the British East India Company defeated Nawab Siraj ud-Daulah of Bengal (who had French support) through a combination of military force and treachery (bribery of Mir Jafar).
- This battle is considered the foundation of British political dominance in India.

**Battle of Buxar (1764):**
- British forces defeated the combined armies of Nawab Mir Qasim, the Nawab of Awadh, and the Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II.
- The Treaty of Allahabad (1765) gave the British the diwani (right to collect revenue) of Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa — transforming them from traders to rulers.

**Expansion Across India:**
- **Mysore Wars (1767–1799):** Four wars against Hyder Ali and his son **Tipu Sultan** — the "Tiger of Mysore" — who was killed at the fall of Seringapatam (1799). Tipu was the first Indian ruler to ally with France against the British and introduced rocket artillery into warfare.
- **Anglo-Maratha Wars (1775–1818):** Three wars ending in British control of all Maratha territories.
- **Anglo-Sikh Wars (1845–49):** Annexation of Punjab.
- **Doctrine of Lapse (Lord Dalhousie, 1848):** British policy of annexing princely states with no male heir — used to annex Satara, Jhansi, Nagpur, and others.

---

### 3.3 The Great Revolt of 1857

Also called the **Sepoy Mutiny** by the British and the **First War of Indian Independence** by Indian nationalists.

**Causes:**
- Immediate: Introduction of the Enfield rifle with cartridges rumoured to be greased with pig and cow fat, offensive to Muslim and Hindu sepoys respectively.
- Deeper causes: Annexations under Doctrine of Lapse, religious interference fears, economic exploitation, and discontent among Indian soldiers.

**Key Events and Leaders:**
- Began at Meerut on May 10, 1857; spread rapidly to Delhi, Agra, Kanpur, Lucknow, Jhansi, and Bareilly.
- **Bahadur Shah Zafar II** was proclaimed the symbolic leader.
- **Rani Lakshmibai of Jhansi:** Fought heroically against the British and died in battle at Gwalior in 1858 — one of the most celebrated figures of the uprising.
- **Nana Sahib** led resistance in Kanpur; **Tantia Tope** conducted guerrilla warfare.
- **Begum Hazrat Mahal** led the resistance in Lucknow.

**Outcome:**
- The revolt was crushed by mid-1858.
- The British Crown directly assumed governance of India from the East India Company — beginning the **British Raj** (1858–1947).
- Bahadur Shah Zafar was exiled to Rangoon.

---

### 3.4 British Raj — Impact and Administration

**Economic Impact:**
- The **Drain of Wealth** theory (articulated by Dadabhai Naoroji in Poverty and Un-British Rule in India): Massive transfer of India's wealth to Britain through trade policies, taxation, and debt servicing.
- Deliberate deindustrialization of India's textile industry to benefit British mills in Manchester and Lancashire.
- Construction of railways (from 1853) primarily to transport raw materials and troops, not for Indian welfare.
- Exploitative land revenue systems (Permanent Settlement in Bengal, Ryotwari in Madras and Bombay, Mahalwari in northern India) impoverished peasants.
- Major famines: The Great Bengal Famine (1770) under Company rule killed an estimated 10 million. Under the Raj, famines in 1876–78, 1896–97, and 1943 Bengal Famine (2–3 million dead) — largely caused or worsened by British policies.

**Social and Cultural Impact:**
- Introduction of English education (Lord Macaulay's Minute, 1835) created an English-educated Indian class.
- Social reforms by British governors: abolition of sati (1829, Lord William Bentinck), suppression of Thuggee, widow remarriage (1856).
- Emergence of a new middle class and English-speaking intelligentsia who would lead the independence movement.
- Christian missionary activity and printing presses accelerated literacy and social reform.

---

### 3.5 Indian Renaissance and Early Nationalism

**Social and Religious Reform Movements:**
- **Raja Ram Mohan Roy (1772–1833):** Founded the Brahmo Samaj (1828); campaigned against sati, child marriage, and caste discrimination. Called the "Father of the Indian Renaissance."
- **Swami Dayananda Saraswati (1824–1883):** Founded the Arya Samaj (1875); promoted Vedic education and Hindu revival; opposed idol worship and caste discrimination.
- **Swami Vivekananda (1863–1902):** Disciple of Ramakrishna Paramahamsa; represented India at the 1893 Parliament of World's Religions in Chicago; inspired a generation of nationalists with his call for "strength" as religion.
- **Jyotirao Phule (1827–1890):** Maharashtra-based social reformer who fought against Brahmanical dominance and caste oppression; founded the Satyashodhak Samaj.
- **Sir Syed Ahmad Khan (1817–1898):** Advocated modern scientific education for Muslims; founded the Muhammadan Anglo-Oriental College at Aligarh (later Aligarh Muslim University).

**Early Political Nationalism:**
- **Indian National Congress (INC)** founded in 1885 by A. O. Hume (a British civil servant) with Dadabhai Naoroji, Gopal Krishna Gokhale, and Bal Gangadhar Tilak as key early leaders.
- Initially sought constitutional reforms within the British Empire; gradually moved toward demands for self-rule.
- **Bal Gangadhar Tilak (1856–1920):** "Lokmanya" Tilak popularized the Ganesh Chaturthi festival as a tool for mass mobilization and asserted "Swaraj is my birthright and I shall have it!" He led the Swadeshi movement in Maharashtra.
- **Partition of Bengal (1905)** by Lord Curzon sparked the Swadeshi Movement — boycott of British goods and promotion of Indian-made products — igniting mass nationalism.
- **Muslim League** founded in 1906, initially to protect Muslim political interests within British India.
- **Morley-Minto Reforms (1909):** Introduced separate electorates for Muslims — a policy that deepened communal divisions.

---

### 3.6 The Gandhian Era and the Independence Movement (1919–1947)

**Mahatma Gandhi (1869–1948):**
Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi returned from South Africa in 1915 after leading successful civil rights campaigns there. He transformed the freedom movement into a mass movement rooted in non-violence (ahimsa) and truth (Satyagraha).

**Jallianwala Bagh Massacre (1919):**
British Brigadier-General Reginald Dyer ordered troops to open fire on an unarmed crowd gathered at Jallianwala Bagh, Amritsar, during Baisakhi. Hundreds (officially 379, estimated over 1,000) were killed. The massacre shocked the nation and radicalized the independence movement. Rabindranath Tagore renounced his British knighthood in protest.

**Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–22):**
Gandhi launched the first major mass movement — Indians were to boycott British institutions (courts, schools, civil services) and return honors. Ended abruptly after the Chauri Chaura incident (1922), where a mob burned a police station, killing 22 officers.

**Simon Commission (1927) and Nehru Report (1928):**
The all-British Simon Commission (sent to review India's constitutional status) was boycotted across India with "Simon Go Back" protests. The Congress responded with the Nehru Report proposing dominion status and fundamental rights.

**Civil Disobedience Movement and Dandi Salt March (1930):**
Gandhi led a 240-mile march from Sabarmati Ashram to Dandi (Gujarat) to make salt from seawater, defying the British salt monopoly. This act of civil disobedience electrified India and the world, becoming one of history's most iconic acts of peaceful protest.

**Gandhi-Irwin Pact (1931)** and **Round Table Conferences (1930–32):** Negotiations between Gandhi and the British produced no significant results on self-government.

**Government of India Act (1935):** Provided for provincial autonomy and a federal structure; significant but fell far short of full independence.

**Key Leaders of the Independence Movement:**

- **Jawaharlal Nehru (1889–1964):** Cambridge-educated barrister; Gandhi's chosen political heir; envisioned a secular, socialist, democratic India. Led the Congress from the 1930s onward; authored The Discovery of India while imprisoned.
- **Subhas Chandra Bose (1897–1945):** "Netaji." Twice elected Congress president but clashed with Gandhi over methods. Escaped British house arrest and traveled to Germany and Japan during World War II. Formed the **Indian National Army (INA / Azad Hind Fauj)** from Indian prisoners of war to fight the British. His slogan: "Give me blood, and I shall give you freedom!" Died under mysterious circumstances in 1945.
- **Bhagat Singh (1907–1931):** Revolutionary socialist who shot British officer John Saunders (mistaking him for the man responsible for Lala Lajpat Rai's death) and bombed the Central Legislative Assembly with Batukeshwar Dutt to "make the deaf hear." Hanged at age 23 alongside Sukhdev and Rajguru; became an immortal martyr and symbol of revolutionary nationalism.
- **Chandrashekhar Azad (1906–1931):** Another revolutionary who famously shot himself rather than surrender to the British.
- **Dr. B. R. Ambedkar (1891–1956):** Born into a Dalit family, Ambedkar became one of India's greatest scholars (PhD from Columbia and LSE). He fought relentlessly for the rights of Dalits and marginalized communities, organized the Mahad Satyagraha (1927), publicly burned the Manu Smriti, and later converted to Buddhism with 500,000 followers. Appointed India's first Law Minister; chief architect of the Indian Constitution.
- **Maulana Abul Kalam Azad:** A Muslim cleric and Congress leader who opposed partition and championed Hindu-Muslim unity.
- **Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel (1875–1950):** The "Iron Man of India." Organized the Bardoli Satyagraha (1928). After independence, he led the political integration of 562 princely states into the Indian Union — a diplomatic and strategic achievement of extraordinary magnitude.
- **C. Rajagopalachari (Rajaji), Rajendra Prasad, Sarojini Naidu** (first woman Governor of a state), **Annie Besant**, **Lala Lajpat Rai** (the "Lion of Punjab"), and many others contributed immensely.

**Quit India Movement (1942):**
At the height of World War II, Gandhi launched the most forceful movement yet: "Do or Die." The British immediately arrested virtually the entire Congress leadership. Mass uprisings, strikes, and sabotage spread across India. Though suppressed, it demonstrated the British that governing India was no longer sustainable.

**INA Trials (1945):**
The British decision to try INA officers after the war backfired spectacularly — the trials generated enormous public sympathy for the defendants and galvanized the independence movement further.

**Royal Indian Navy Mutiny (1946):**
Sailors in the Royal Indian Navy revolted, signaling to Britain that it could no longer rely on Indian armed forces to hold the country.

---

### 3.7 Partition and Independence (1947)

**Two-Nation Theory and the Muslim League:**
Muhammad Ali Jinnah and the Muslim League argued that Hindus and Muslims were two separate nations requiring separate homelands. The Congress, led by Gandhi and Nehru, opposed partition.

**Mountbatten Plan (June 1947):**
The last Viceroy, Lord Mountbatten, announced the partition of British India into two independent dominions — India and Pakistan — to take effect on August 14–15, 1947.

**The Partition:**
- The boundary was drawn hastily by Cyril Radcliffe (who had never visited India) within five weeks — the Radcliffe Line.
- Pakistan comprised West Pakistan (present-day Pakistan) and East Pakistan (present-day Bangladesh).
- The partition triggered one of the largest mass migrations in human history: an estimated 10–20 million people crossed the border.
- Devastating communal violence — massacres, rapes, abductions — killed between 200,000 and 2 million people.
- Gandhi, deeply heartbroken by the violence, undertook fasts to stop the killings.

**Independence:**
- **August 15, 1947:** India became independent. Nehru delivered his iconic "Tryst with Destiny" speech at midnight in Parliament.
- **January 30, 1948:** Mahatma Gandhi was assassinated by Nathuram Godse, a Hindu nationalist who blamed him for the partition. The nation mourned its "Father of the Nation."

---

## 4. Modern India (1947–Present)

### 4.1 Republic of India and the Constitution (1950)

India became a republic on **January 26, 1950**, when the **Constitution of India** came into force — the world's longest written constitution.

- **Dr. B. R. Ambedkar** chaired the drafting committee.
- Guaranteed Fundamental Rights (equality, freedom, protection against exploitation, religious freedom, cultural rights, constitutional remedies).
- Adopted a Parliamentary Democracy, a Federal structure with a strong centre, an independent judiciary, and universal adult suffrage.
- Declared India a Sovereign, Democratic, Republic (the words "Socialist" and "Secular" were added by the 42nd Amendment in 1976).
- **Dr. Rajendra Prasad** became the first President of India.
- **Jawaharlal Nehru** became the first Prime Minister.

---

### 4.2 Nehru Era — Nation Building (1947–1964)

Nehru's vision for India was that of a modern, secular, democratic, socialist nation.

**Key Developments:**
- **Five-Year Plans:** Nehru adopted central economic planning with five-year plans modelled on the Soviet experience. Focus on heavy industry, dams (called "temples of modern India"), and self-reliance.
- **Industrialization:** Establishment of public sector steel plants at Bhilai (with Soviet help), Rourkela (with German help), and Durgapur (with British help).
- **Education:** Founding of IITs (first at Kharagpur, 1951), IIMs, AIIMS, and other national institutions.
- **Panchayati Raj:** Democratic local self-governance at the village level introduced.
- **Land Reforms:** Zamindari abolition across most states; redistribution of agricultural land.
- **Non-Aligned Movement (NAM):** Nehru, along with Egypt's Nasser and Yugoslavia's Tito, founded the Non-Aligned Movement (1961), keeping India independent of the US and USSR cold war blocs.
- **Integration of States:** Sardar Patel's extraordinary effort integrated 562 princely states into India. Hyderabad was annexed by police action in September 1948; Junagadh and Kashmir also integrated (the latter remains disputed).
- **States Reorganization Act (1956):** States reorganized along linguistic lines, creating Maharashtra, Gujarat, Andhra Pradesh, Kerala, etc.
- **Sino-Indian War (1962):** China's surprise attack on India's Himalayan borders resulted in a humiliating Indian defeat. India lost territory in Aksai Chin. This shattered Nehru's "Hindi-Chini bhai bhai" (India-China are brothers) vision and severely damaged his health and reputation. Nehru died in May 1964.

---

### 4.3 Lal Bahadur Shastri and the 1965 War

**Lal Bahadur Shastri (1964–1966):**
- Succeeded Nehru as PM. Quiet but decisive leader.
- **Indo-Pakistani War (1965):** Pakistan launched Operation Gibraltar and Grand Slam in Jammu & Kashmir. India responded with a full-scale offensive across the international border, including armoured battles near Lahore. The war ended in a stalemate; the **Tashkent Declaration (1966)** restored the status quo. Shastri died in Tashkent under mysterious circumstances the day after signing the agreement.
- Gave India the inspiring slogan: "Jai Jawan, Jai Kisan" (Hail the Soldier, Hail the Farmer).
- The Green Revolution, spearheaded by M. S. Swaminathan with high-yielding varieties of wheat and rice, transformed India from a food-deficit to a food-surplus nation.

---

### 4.4 Indira Gandhi Era (1966–1984)

**Indira Gandhi (1966–1977 and 1980–1984):**
Daughter of Jawaharlal Nehru; one of the most powerful and controversial leaders in Indian history.

**First Term (1966–1977):**
- **Bank Nationalization (1969):** Nationalized 14 major banks to bring credit to the poor and strengthen state control.
- **Green Revolution:** Food self-sufficiency achieved.
- **Indo-Pakistani War (1971) and Liberation of Bangladesh:** Pakistan's brutal military crackdown in East Pakistan (Operation Searchlight) created 10 million refugees. India, under Indira Gandhi, signed a Treaty of Friendship with the USSR and intervened militarily. The Indian Army, in one of its greatest victories, accepted the surrender of 93,000 Pakistani troops — the largest military surrender since World War II. East Pakistan became independent **Bangladesh** on December 16, 1971. Indira Gandhi was hailed as "Durga" and "Iron Lady."
- **Smiling Buddha (1974):** India conducted its first nuclear weapons test at Pokhran, Rajasthan, becoming the world's sixth nuclear-capable nation.
- **Emergency (1975–1977):** Citing internal disturbance and court orders threatening her position, Indira Gandhi declared a State of Emergency on June 25, 1975. Civil liberties were suspended, press censorship imposed, political opponents (including opposition leaders Jayaprakash Narayan, Atal Bihari Vajpayee) arrested, and forced sterilization programs launched under Sanjay Gandhi. The Emergency remains the darkest chapter in post-independence Indian democracy. She lifted it in 1977 and called elections — which the Congress lost to the Janata Party coalition.

**Second Term (1980–1984):**
- Returned to power in 1980 after the Janata government collapsed.
- **Operation Blue Star (June 1984):** To flush out Sikh militants led by Jarnail Singh Bhindranwale who had fortified the Golden Temple in Amritsar, Indira Gandhi ordered a military operation. The operation caused heavy casualties and desecrated the holiest Sikh shrine, deeply alienating the Sikh community.
- **Assassination (October 31, 1984):** Indira Gandhi was assassinated by two of her Sikh bodyguards in retaliation for Operation Blue Star. Anti-Sikh riots followed, killing approximately 3,000 Sikhs — primarily in Delhi.

---

### 4.5 Rajiv Gandhi Era (1984–1989)

**Rajiv Gandhi (1984–1989):**
- Eldest son of Indira Gandhi; became the youngest Prime Minister of India at 40.
- Came to power on a sympathy wave; the Congress won a historic 414 seats in 1984.
- **Computerization and Technology:** Championed information technology, founded the National Informatics Centre, reduced import duties on computers — laying groundwork for India's IT revolution.
- **Accord Agreements:** Signed the Punjab Accord with Harchand Singh Longowal (1985) and the Assam Accord (1985) to resolve regional conflicts.
- **Sri Lanka:** Sent the Indian Peace Keeping Force (IPKF) to Sri Lanka to resolve the Tamil-Sinhalese conflict; the mission became controversial and costly.
- **Bofors Scandal:** Allegations of kickbacks in the purchase of Swedish Bofors artillery guns severely damaged his reputation.
- Lost the 1989 elections; **V. P. Singh** became PM.
- **Assassination (May 21, 1991):** Rajiv Gandhi was assassinated by an LTTE (Tamil Tigers) suicide bomber at Sriperumbudur, Tamil Nadu, during the election campaign.

---

### 4.6 Economic Liberalization (1991) — India's Transformation

India faced a severe balance-of-payments crisis in 1991 — foreign exchange reserves fell to just two weeks' worth of imports. India was forced to pledge 67 tonnes of gold to the Bank of England and IMF as collateral.

Under **Prime Minister P. V. Narasimha Rao** and **Finance Minister Dr. Manmohan Singh**, India launched sweeping economic reforms:
- **Liberalization:** Dismantled the License Raj, reduced bureaucratic control over industry.
- **Privatization:** Disinvestment from state-owned enterprises.
- **Globalization:** Opened markets to foreign investment and trade.

The reforms transformed India's economy. GDP growth accelerated. A booming IT and software export industry emerged, centered in **Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Chennai, and Pune**, making India a global technology hub. Companies like Infosys, Wipro, and TCS became global names.

---

### 4.7 Babri Masjid Demolition and Communal Tensions (1992)

On December 6, 1992, a mob of Hindu nationalists demolished the 16th-century Babri Masjid in Ayodhya, claiming it stood on the birthplace of Lord Ram. The demolition triggered widespread communal riots across India, killing approximately 2,000 people. The Mumbai serial blasts of March 1993 followed. The dispute reached the Supreme Court; in November 2019 the court ruled in favour of a Hindu temple at the site. The Ram Mandir (Ram Temple) was inaugurated at Ayodhya on **January 22, 2024**.

---

### 4.8 Pokhran II and India as a Nuclear Power (1998)

Under **Prime Minister Atal Bihari Vajpayee**, India conducted five nuclear tests at Pokhran (Operation Shakti) in May 1998, declaring itself a nuclear weapons state. Pakistan responded with its own nuclear tests. The US imposed sanctions, which were later lifted.

---

### 4.9 Kargil War (1999)

Pakistani soldiers and militants infiltrated across the Line of Control into the Kargil district of Jammu & Kashmir during winter. India launched **Operation Vijay** to recapture the peaks. After intense high-altitude warfare with significant casualties on both sides, India successfully recaptured most positions by July 1999. The war ended with Pakistan withdrawing. It was the first armed conflict between two nuclear powers.

---

### 4.10 UPA Era — Growth and Welfare (2004–2014)

**Dr. Manmohan Singh (2004–2014):**
- India experienced GDP growth rates of 8–9% during 2004–2008, among the highest in the world.
- **MNREGA (2005):** Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act — guaranteed 100 days of employment per year to rural households; largest public works program in the world.
- **RTI Act (2005):** Right to Information Act gave citizens the right to access government information — a landmark transparency measure.
- **Nuclear Deal (2008):** India-US Civil Nuclear Agreement (123 Agreement) ended India's nuclear isolation.
- The global financial crisis of 2008 slowed growth but India recovered quickly.
- Second term plagued by major corruption scandals (2G Spectrum, Commonwealth Games, Coalgate), weakening the government.

---

### 4.11 NDA Era and Narendra Modi (2014–Present)

**Narendra Modi** led the BJP to a massive majority (282 seats) in 2014 — the first single-party majority in 30 years — and won again in 2019 (303 seats). In 2024, the BJP won 240 seats and formed the NDA government for a third consecutive term.

**Key Policies and Developments:**
- **Swachh Bharat Mission (2014):** Mass sanitation campaign; construction of over 100 million toilets.
- **Make in India:** Initiative to boost domestic manufacturing.
- **Jan Dhan Yojana:** Financial inclusion scheme opened over 500 million bank accounts for the unbanked.
- **Demonetization (November 2016):** Rs. 500 and Rs. 1,000 notes (86% of currency in circulation) were demonetized overnight to curb black money. Caused massive short-term economic disruption; long-term impact debated.
- **GST (Goods and Services Tax, 2017):** Replaced India's complex multi-tax structure with a unified national tax system.
- **Surgical Strikes (2016):** Indian Army conducted cross-border operations against terrorist launch pads in Pakistan-administered Kashmir following the Uri attack.
- **Balakot Air Strike (2019):** India conducted air strikes on a terrorist camp in Balakot, Pakistan — the first such strike since 1971.
- **Abrogation of Article 370 (August 5, 2019):** Removed the special autonomous status of Jammu & Kashmir and bifurcated the state into two Union Territories: Jammu & Kashmir and Ladakh.
- **CAA (Citizenship Amendment Act, 2019):** Offered expedited citizenship to persecuted religious minorities (Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, Christians) from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan. Sparked nationwide protests.
- **COVID-19 Pandemic (2020–21):** India managed two devastating waves. India's vaccination program — one of the largest in the world — administered over 2 billion doses of Covishield and Covaxin.
- **Ram Mandir Inauguration (January 22, 2024):** The Ram Mandir at Ayodhya was inaugurated — a historic moment for millions of Hindus.

## 5. India's Scientific and Space Achievements

- **ISRO (Indian Space Research Organisation):** Founded 1969; one of the world's leading space agencies.
- **Aryabhata (1975):** India's first satellite.
- **SLV-3 (1980):** India's first indigenously developed satellite launch vehicle.
- **Chandrayaan-1 (2008):** India's first lunar mission; discovered water molecules on the Moon's surface.
- **Mangalyaan / Mars Orbiter Mission (2014):** India became the first Asian nation and the first in the world to reach Mars orbit on its first attempt, at a fraction of the cost of comparable missions.
- **Chandrayaan-3 (August 23, 2023):** India became the **first country in the world to land near the lunar south pole**, and the fourth country overall to achieve a soft Moon landing. The lander was named Vikram and the rover Pragyan.
- **Gaganyaan:** India's upcoming human spaceflight mission, aiming to send Indian astronauts (vyomanauts) to space.
- **AstroSat:** India's first dedicated astronomy satellite (2015).
- **NavIC:** India's own regional navigation satellite system.

## 7. Key Historical Concepts in Indian Context

- **Dharma:** The moral and cosmic order; central to Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain worldviews.
- **Ahimsa:** Non-violence; a principle that shaped Gandhian philosophy and Indian statecraft.
- **Swaraj:** Self-rule; the central demand of the independence movement.
- **Secularism:** India's constitutional commitment to equal treatment of all religions by the state.
- **Federalism:** Division of powers between the central government and states.
- **Panchayati Raj:** System of democratic governance at the grassroots/village level.

## Summary

India's history is a 5,000-year tapestry woven from countless threads — the mathematical genius of Aryabhata, the moral courage of Ashoka, the architectural splendour of Shah Jahan, the revolutionary fire of Bhagat Singh, the spiritual non-violence of Mahatma Gandhi, and the democratic aspirations of B. R. Ambedkar. From the world's earliest urban civilization to a rapidly rising 21st-century global power, India's story is one of extraordinary resilience, diversity, and civilizational continuity — a living testament to the idea that unity in diversity is not just a slogan, but a way of life.
